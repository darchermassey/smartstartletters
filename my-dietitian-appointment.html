My dietitian appointment

Key points about your dietitian appointment

  • Dietitians are registered health professionals and experts in food and nutrition.
  • Dietitians complete specialised university training and are regulated by the New Zealand Dietitians Board. This makes them different from nutritionists.
  • Dietitians provide science-based nutrition advice to help people improve their health and wellbeing.
  • If you are referred to see a dietitian you may have to wait a while before you can see one. This page provides information about how you can prepare for your appointment and what to do while you wait.

Dietitians are registered health professionals who are qualified experts in food and nutrition. Dietitians work in a variety of settings, including hospitals and community health services. In your appointment, they can support you with your overall health and lifestyle goals. Dietitians can help you find practical ways to include nutritious foods in your diet that can make a real difference to how you feel now and in the future.

The first appointment with a dietitian usually lasts 45 to 60 minutes. This gives your dietitian plenty of time to get to know you. 

Follow-up appointments are usually slightly shorter – about 15 to 30 minutes. 

During your appointment, your dietitian might ask about:

  • your health goals and how they can support you
  • your health needs
  • your lifestyle and whānau
  • the foods you enjoy
  • what you usually eat.

Together, you'll set achievable goals that fit your life and reflect what matters most to you. It's not about strict rules. It's about making changes that feel possible, are meaningful for you and are sustainable so that you can keep going with the changes you make. 

Image credit: Smart Start Project

While you wait for your first appointment, there are some simple ways to get ready.

  • Think about or write down any questions you have, such as any symptoms you're experiencing, foods or lifestyle habits.
  • Think about what you usually eat. Your dietitian will ask you about what you usually eat and drink so that they can provide practical suggestions that work for you. You might be asked:
    • what you usually eat and drink for breakfast, lunch, dinner or snacks
    • if there are any foods you enjoy or avoid
    • if you're following a certain diet.

Being prepared can help you get the most of your appointment.


Should I bring anything to my appointment?

You don’t need to bring anything unless your dietitian asks. But, it can be helpful to bring your list of questions and the names of medicines or supplements if you take any regularly.


Support person

You are welcome to bring a support person to your appointment. If someone in your household usually does the cooking or grocery shopping, it can be helpful to have them join in person, by phone or video call (if your dietitian is okay with it). Having a support person can also help you feel more comfortable and remember what was discussed.


Did your dietitian mention a food diary? 

Not all dietitians ask for one. If yours hasn’t mentioned it, don’t worry. If they have asked you to keep a food diary this is just a record of everything you eat and drink over a period of time, often 3 days. 

Here's an example of a food diary. Note that you don’t need to change what you eat when you're keeping a food diary.

 

Making goals

You may have a goal in mind when you're going to see a dietitian, for example eating more healthily, losing or gaining weight, or wanting to avoid certain foods. If not, your dietitian may work with you to set a goal and make a plan for how to achieve it. Either way it can help if you understand about how to make a goal that's meaningful to you and how to break it down into simple, manageable steps. Read more about goal setting and making changes. 

Dietitians

Registered dietitians are the only registered nutrition professional in Aotearoa New Zealand legally recognised by the New Zealand government (under the HPCA Act). 

Dietitians complete a 3 year undergraduate Health Science degree plus a 2 year Master of Dietetics degree (or equivalent), approved by the New Zealand Dietitians Board. 


Nutritionists

Nutritionists in Aotearoa New Zealand can register with the Nutrition Society of Aotearoa New Zealand (which requires at least an undergraduate degree), however the title 'nutritionist' is not legally protected. This means not all nutritionists are registered or qualified, and some may have little or no formal training. Always check a nutritionist’s qualifications and whether they are registered with the Nutrition Society of New Zealand.

There are many reasons why someone might be referred to a dietitian.

Dietitians can help with a wide range of health concerns including:

  • high blood pressure
  • high cholesterol
  • unintentional weight loss or poor appetite
  • food allergies or intolerances
  • coeliac disease
  • irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) or other gut issues
  • nutrition during pregnancy
  • diabetes
  • weight management

Your healthcare provider or another health professional may have referred you because they believe a dietitian can support your health and wellbeing. 

The New Zealand health system is under a lot of pressure right now, so wait times for appointments can sometimes be long.

How long you wait can depend on a lot of things, but every team is doing their best to see you as soon as they can. When your dietitian is ready to see you, they will be in touch. We understand waiting can feel stressful or worrying. While you wait, we hope this page gives you some helpful information to support you. 

If your health gets worse or you need help urgently please contact your healthcare provider.

Even small changes can make a big difference to your health and wellbeing. 

During your appointment, your dietitian will work with you to set practical, achievable goals that fit your lifestyle and support positive changes relevant to your health.

Unintentional weight loss

If you are losing weight without trying, it’s important to keep your strength and energy up. Try eating small meals or snacks every 2 to 3 hours throughout the day.

If you have more complex health needs, such as irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) or chronic kidney disease, it may be best to wait until you see your dietitian before introducing changes. But start thinking about what changes might be realistic for you.
